1. Overview and goal

Given the isoform-proportion data (IPD) produced by a splicing system, we want to recover the underlying kinetic rates. Whether that's possible — and with which solver — depends on the splicing regime the gene obeys. A regime here is any structural prior on the rate vector and the active subgraph: topology constraints, pooling of rates across junctions, how much competition is allowed at branching states, and so on.

We package a regime as a splicing regime vector $\theta = (\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots)$ — see step 2. Every gene is assumed to sit at some point in $\theta$-space; observing an IPD is an observation about which region its $\theta$ is in; and for every $\theta$ region, one of the five solvers (step 10) is best in a sharp theoretical sense.

The goal of this sandbox is modest and deliberate: identify the smallest set of SRV coordinates that cover the splicing regimes we care about, and characterise where analytic direct inference (NNLS and its regularised variants) succeeds and where it fails.

2. The Splicing Regime Vector θ

The SRV is a compact parametric description of the prior on the splicing rate vector. Fixing $\theta$ determines (i) which subgraph of the DADA graph is active, (ii) how rates distribute on active edges, and (iii) how much variation there is between junctions.

$$ \theta \;=\; (\eta,\, \sigma,\, \alpha,\, K) \;\in\; [0,1] \times \mathbb{R}_+ \times \mathbb{R}_+ \times [0,1]. $$

Three active axes plus one reserved. Every axis is orthogonal in the sense that it moves a distinct statistic of the generated rate vector: $\sigma$ perturbs per-junction amplitudes, $\eta$ perturbs the active-subgraph support, and $\alpha$ perturbs the within-child weight distribution.

  • $\eta \in [0, 1]$ — structure. Fraction of parents active per child: $k = \max(1,\,\mathrm{round}(\eta \cdot m))$. The active subset of size $k$ is drawn uniformly from the $m$ parents. $\eta = 0 \Rightarrow k = 1$ (strict tree); $\eta = 1 \Rightarrow k = m$ (full graph).
  • $\sigma \in [0, \infty)$ — spread. Log-normal scatter of junction amplitudes $\rho_j = R_0 \cdot \exp(\sigma \cdot \mathcal{N}(0,1))$. Independent across junctions.
  • $\alpha \in (0, \infty)$ — concentration. Dirichlet concentration for the active-parent weights $(w_i)_{i \in \mathcal{S}_c} \sim \mathrm{Dir}(\alpha, \ldots, \alpha)$. $\alpha \to 0$ one parent dominates; $\alpha \to \infty$ uniform weights. The UI slider maps $\log_{10}\alpha \in [-2, 2]$.
  • $K$ — reserved. Placeholder axis, currently a no-op. Candidates for later: a kinetic-fraction axis, a per-edge log-normal jitter $\sigma_r$, or observation concentration $\kappa$.

The three UI presets (Predetermined / Mixed / Kinetic) are specific points in $(\eta, \alpha)$-space that reproduce each canonical regime under the single Mixed-Dirichlet sampler:

3. DADA strings and junctions

A DADA string is a sequence of splice-site letters: D is a donor (5′ splice site) and A is an acceptor (3′ splice site). For example, DADADA is a gene with three donors and three acceptors in alternating order along the transcript.

A junction is a pair $(d, a)$ where $d$ is a donor position, $a$ is an acceptor position, and $d < a$. Each junction removes the intron spanning positions $[d, a)$. All valid junctions are enumerated as

$$ \mathcal{J} \;=\; \bigl\{\, (i, j) \;:\; \mathrm{site}_i = \mathtt{D},\ \mathrm{site}_j = \mathtt{A},\ i < j \,\bigr\}. $$

Compatibility

Two junctions can coexist in the same mature isoform iff their half-open intervals are disjoint and they don't share a splice site:

$$ \mathrm{compatible}\bigl((d_1, a_1),\, (d_2, a_2)\bigr) \;\iff\; d_1 \neq d_2 \,\land\, a_1 \neq a_2 \,\land\, \bigl(a_1 \leq d_2 \,\lor\, a_2 \leq d_1\bigr). $$

Nested junctions (one inside another) are not allowed here — this tool models only strictly sequential splicing along the transcript.

4. Isoform states (the graph)

A state $s$ is a set of compatible junctions already spliced out. The null state $s = \emptyset$ is the unspliced pre-mRNA. A terminal state is one where no further compatible junction can be added — no more splicing is possible.

States and edges are built by breadth-first enumeration:

  1. Start with the null state.
  2. For each state $s$ and each junction $j \notin s$ compatible with all of $s$, create an edge $s \to s \cup \{j\}$.
  3. Every edge is labelled by the junction it adds.

The layer of a state is the number of junctions it contains, i.e. how far along the splicing cascade it sits. Layer 0 is null; the highest layer contains the terminal states.

5. Forward model — CTMC steady state

The system is a continuous-time Markov chain on the isoform state graph. New pre-mRNA appears at the null state at a constant transcription rate (fixed at 1 — it just sets the overall mass scale). From each state, molecules either splice further (to a neighbour) or are exported out of the nucleus and counted as that isoform.

Let $p_i$ be the steady-state abundance of state $i$, $r_{i \to j}$ the rate of the edge from $i$ to $j$, and $\lambda_i$ the export rate from state $i$. The steady-state balance, for every state $i$, is

$$ \sum_j r_{j \to i}\, p_j \;=\; \Bigl(\lambda_i + \sum_j r_{i \to j}\Bigr)\, p_i, $$

with an additional $+1$ on the right-hand side of state $0$ to account for transcription. Written as a linear system in the abundance vector $p$:

$$ Q\, p \;=\; -b, \qquad Q_{ii} = -\Bigl(\lambda_i + \sum_j r_{i \to j}\Bigr), \qquad Q_{ji} = r_{i \to j}\ (i \neq j), \qquad b_0 = 1. $$

We solve this directly with Gaussian elimination, clip to non-negatives (numerical safety), and normalise so that $\sum_i p_i = 1$. Every abundance in this tool is therefore a fraction — the isoform-proportion data (IPD).

6. Export kinetics

Export is the process that "measures" an isoform. Once a molecule is exported, it's counted as whatever state it was in at that moment. The export rate $\lambda_i$ depends on how far along the splicing path state $i$ sits. Define the splice-completion coordinate

$$ c_i \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{layer}(i)}{\mathrm{layer}_{\max}} \;\in\; [0, 1], \qquad \lambda_i \;=\; \lambda_{\mathrm{base}} \cdot f(c_i). $$

$\lambda_{\mathrm{base}}$ is pinned at $1.0$ so every rate in the interface is expressed in units of the export rate. The shape $f(c)$ satisfies $f(0) = \mathrm{nullFrac}$ and $f(1) = 1$; you pick its functional form from the export profile dropdown. The available profiles are:

  • constant — $f(c) = 1$ everywhere; export is oblivious to splice state.
  • linear — $f(c) = \mathrm{nullFrac} + (1 - \mathrm{nullFrac}) \cdot c$.
  • parabolic — $f(c) = \mathrm{nullFrac} + (1 - \mathrm{nullFrac}) \cdot c^2$; commitment ramps up.
  • exponential — $f(c) = \mathrm{nullFrac} + (1 - \mathrm{nullFrac}) \cdot (e^{3c} - 1)/(e^{3} - 1)$; sharp commitment near the end.
  • sigmoid — switch-like, transitions around $c = 0.5$.

nullFrac controls how readily unspliced RNA escapes the nucleus. When $\mathrm{nullFrac} \approx 0$, only fully spliced products are exported; when $\mathrm{nullFrac} = 1$, export is constant.

Remark. The IPD is biased by the export shape. The same set of splicing rates produces different IPDs if the export profile changes, and the inverse solver assumes it knows $f(c)$. "Model mismatch" in this tool usually means simulating with one profile and solving as if another were in effect.

7. The inverse problem

The observations are the IPDs $p_i$. The unknowns are the edge rates $r_k$. The steady-state equation rearranges into a linear system in $r$ alone: for each non-source state $i$,

$$ p_i \cdot \Bigl(\lambda_i + \sum_{k:\,\mathrm{src}(k) = i} r_k\Bigr) \;=\; \sum_{k:\,\mathrm{dst}(k) = i} r_k\, p_{\mathrm{src}(k)}. $$

Moving everything to one side gives a linear system $A\, r = c$ with

$$ A_{i, k} \;=\; \begin{cases} +\, p_{\mathrm{src}(k)} & \text{if edge } k \text{ ends at } i, \\ -\, p_{\mathrm{src}(k)} & \text{if edge } k \text{ starts at } i, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise}, \end{cases} \qquad c_i \;=\; \lambda_i\, p_i. $$

Two subtleties in how we actually build $A$ and $c$:

  • Only columns for edges whose source state has observed non-zero abundance ($p_{\mathrm{src}} > 0$) are included — an edge that starts nowhere can't appear in the data.
  • A row $i$ is dropped from the solve if $i$ is a pure exit — no included edge ends at it. Its balance equation is empty and would be numerically degenerate.

We solve $A\, r = c$ subject to $r \geq 0$ using non-negative least squares (Lawson–Hanson NNLS). Rates can't be negative, and NNLS is the right constrained projection.

8. The Mixed-Dirichlet generator

A single generator parameterised by $\theta = (\eta, \sigma, \alpha)$ produces every regime the sandbox can show. The three UI presets are canonical points in $(\eta, \alpha)$-space; the sliders move freely in between.

Stage 0 — junction amplitudes (σ)

$$ \rho_j \;\sim\; R_0 \cdot \exp\bigl(\sigma \cdot \mathcal{N}(0, 1)\bigr), \qquad R_0 = 75, \qquad j = 1, \ldots, K_{\mathrm{junc}}. $$

Stage 1 — active subgraph (η)

For each child state $c$ with $m$ parents, choose the active-parent count and a uniform subset of that size:

$$ k_c \;=\; \max\!\bigl(1,\ \mathrm{round}(\eta \cdot m)\bigr), \qquad \mathcal{S}_c \;\sim\; \mathrm{UniformSubset}\bigl(\{1, \ldots, m\},\, k_c\bigr). $$

Stage 2 — weights on the active set (α)

Draw a symmetric Dirichlet over the $k_c$ active parents and convert to rates:

$$ (w_i)_{i \in \mathcal{S}_c} \;\sim\; \mathrm{Dirichlet}(\alpha, \ldots, \alpha), \qquad \mathrm{rate}(p_i \to c) \;=\; \begin{cases} \rho_{j(p_i \to c)} \cdot (k_c \cdot w_i), & i \in \mathcal{S}_c, \\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} $$

The factor $k_c$ in front of $w_i$ keeps the expected rate per active edge fixed at $\rho_j$ across the whole $(\eta, \alpha)$-plane. Dropped parents get rate exactly $0$ — they're genuinely absent from the active subgraph.

Preset points.
  • Predetermined — $\eta = 0,\ \alpha = 1$. $k_c = 1$ per child, so $w = 1$ trivially. Strict tree; every active edge has rate $\rho_j$.
  • Mixed — $\eta = 0.5,\ \alpha = 1$. Half the parents active per child. Uniform-on-simplex weights give graded flux splits.
  • Kinetic — $\eta = 1,\ \alpha = 100$. Full graph active; weights $\approx 1/m$ so $\mathrm{rate} \approx \rho_j$. Approaches the pure junction-pooled kinetic limit.
Observation noise. A Dirichlet-$\kappa$ likelihood $y \sim \mathrm{Dirichlet}(10^\kappa \cdot f(r))$ is available in Additional Parameters. At $\kappa = 0$ the sandbox is noiseless (y = f(r) exactly). Larger $\kappa$ = more reads = less noise; $\kappa \gtrsim 4$ is effectively noiseless.

9. The observable mask (dual threshold)

An edge's rate is only recoverable from data if both its source and destination isoforms have measurable abundance. If either $p_{\mathrm{src}}$ or $p_{\mathrm{dst}}$ is effectively zero, the edge contributes nothing the solver can see, and asking about its "rate error" is ill-posed.

Two separate thresholds handle analytics and visualization.

Analytical mask (R² and solver)

$$ \tau_{\mathrm{obs}} \;=\; \max\bigl(10^{-12},\ \textstyle\max_i p_i \cdot 10^{-4}\bigr), \qquad \mathrm{obs}[i] \;=\; \bigl(p_i > \tau_{\mathrm{obs}}\bigr), \qquad \mathrm{obs}[\text{edge}] \;=\; \mathrm{obs}[\mathrm{src}] \land \mathrm{obs}[\mathrm{dst}]. $$

The threshold is relative to the largest observed abundance. This is deliberate: in real measurements, anything four orders of magnitude below the dominant isoform is below the noise floor. It also prevents cascade artefacts — states that are reachable only through a vanishing rate sit at near-machine-precision abundance but aren't actually observable. This strict mask is used for the $R^2$ calculation and the "observable edges" scatter plot.

Visual mask (DAG display)

$$ \tau_{\mathrm{vis}} \;=\; \max\bigl(10^{-14},\ \textstyle\max_i p_i \cdot 10^{-6}\bigr), \qquad \mathrm{vis}[i] \;=\; \bigl(p_i > \tau_{\mathrm{vis}}\bigr), \qquad \mathrm{vis}[\text{edge}] \;=\; \mathrm{vis}[\mathrm{src}] \land \mathrm{vis}[\mathrm{dst}]. $$

The visual mask is $100\times$ more lenient than the analytical mask. By default (Show all nodes & edges off) the DAG uses the visual mask plus a rate-activity check to keep only active, observable edges on screen. Turning the toggle on renders every structural edge — useful when you want to click-and-edit rates on currently-inactive transitions. This prevents a common rendering artefact: with sparse, high-spread parameter settings, intermediate states can have abundances that are below the strict analytical threshold but still represent real pathway nodes. Hiding them disconnects the visible graph into disjoint clusters. The lenient visual mask keeps the DAG topologically connected while still hiding truly negligible states.

The analytical mask (strict) controls the $R^2$ calculation, the recovery table, and the "observable edges" scatter plot. The visual mask (lenient) controls only the DAG rendering. See step 11.

10. Solvers, regularisation, and identifiability

The three solver modes (oracle / θ-aware / naive)

Every solver below can be run in one of three modes, controlled by the Mode selector. These are not different algorithms — they are different information the solver receives about the regime:

Oracle mode. The solver receives the generator's activeMask — the specific edges that carry non-zero rate in this draw. The solver's job reduces to fitting numbers on a known support.

Oracle mode cheats: the active subgraph is part of the latent state we'd want to infer. Not a defensible answer to "given y, what is r?" Useful only as a visualisation of the upper bound — what each solver could achieve if we already knew the subgraph.
θ-aware mode (the honest middle ground). The solver is given $\theta = (\eta, \sigma, \alpha, K)$ but not the realisation's activeMask. This is what a real Bayesian inference at known $\theta$ would do.

Implementation: iterative hard thresholding (IHT). For each child state $c$ with $m_c$ parents, $\eta$ specifies the cardinality $k_c = \max(1, \mathrm{round}(\eta \cdot m_c))$ of the active set; the solver iteratively
  1. solves NNLS on the current candidate active set,
  2. retains the top-$k_c$ incoming edges per child by recovered rate,
  3. repeats until the mask is stable.
The first iteration uses junction-shrinkage at small λ (overdetermined, unique solution) so the thresholding has dense magnitudes to rank; later iterations refine. IHT discovers which $k_c$ edges are active from the IPD alone.
Naive mode. The solver receives only the topology and $p_{\mathrm{obs}}$ — no $\theta$, no activeMask. This is what raw NNLS does without any prior structure.

Naive mode is what you'd get if you walked into the inverse problem cold. It serves as the lower bound: anything any of our solvers achieve over naive is the value of the prior information.

What the three modes tell you

The R² gap between modes is informative:

  • oracle − θ-aware = how much the realisation's specific active subgraph helps beyond just knowing the regime. A small gap means η-IHT is recovering the structure well; a large gap means there are many ways to fit y consistent with η, and we'd need something stronger (more data, posterior inference) to pin down which.
  • θ-aware − naive = how much θ is worth as prior information. A large gap means θ is informative; a small gap means even with θ we can't separate the answer from noise.
  • oracle − naive = the total cost of not knowing anything about the regime.

The honest interpretation: θ-aware does most of the work that oracle does, except in the Predetermined corner where the active tree's exact identity matters — and that gap is the cardinality-shell ambiguity discussed in §14, which a single noiseless IPD cannot resolve.

The five solvers (used inside any mode)

Five solver variants are implemented, all framed as optimisations over the per-edge rate vector $r \in \mathbb{R}_+^M$. Each one is appropriate in a specific region of $\theta$-space; picking the wrong solver for a given prior is the primary failure mode beyond true non-identifiability.

Per-edge NNLS. \[ \widehat r \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{r \geq 0} \; \bigl\lVert A\, r - y \bigr\rVert_2^2. \] Lawson–Hanson active-set solver. Appropriate when the prior yields an identifiable per-edge system (Predetermined on a tree). In underdetermined cases it returns a sparse vertex of the feasible polytope; that sparsity is an algorithmic artefact, not a principled prior.
Pooled NNLS. Columns of $A$ corresponding to edges of the same junction are summed, reducing the unknown from $M$ per-edge rates to $K_{\mathrm{junc}}$ junction rates: \[ \widetilde A_{\cdot, j} \;=\; \sum_{k:\, j(k) = j} A_{\cdot, k}, \qquad \widehat\rho \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\rho \geq 0} \; \lVert \widetilde A\, \rho - y \rVert_2^2, \qquad \widehat r_k \;=\; \widehat\rho_{j(k)}. \] Appropriate when the prior is Pure kinetic (exact). Otherwise imposes an incorrect junction-pooling assumption on per-edge truth.
Junction-shrinkage NNLS (structural, $\lambda$-tunable). Soft junction-pooling. Penalises within-junction rate variance, but doesn't force edges of the same junction to share a rate: \[ \widehat r \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{r \geq 0} \; \bigl\lVert A\, r - y \bigr\rVert_2^2 \,+\, \lambda \sum_{j = 1}^{K_{\mathrm{junc}}} \sum_{k:\, j(k) = j} \bigl(r_k - \rho_j(r)\bigr)^2, \qquad \rho_j(r) \;=\; \frac{1}{n_j}\sum_{k:\, j(k) = j} r_k. \]

What the penalty does. For each junction $j$, it pulls the rates of the edges that use $j$ toward their own average. $\lambda = 0$ is per-edge NNLS (no pulling); $\lambda \to \infty$ forces all edges of a junction to be equal (pooled NNLS). Intermediate $\lambda$ is a principled interpolation that encodes the biology directly: "the same splice site tends to have similar kinetics, but the exact rate can vary by context."

Why the Lawson–Hanson sparsity artefact disappears. Vanilla per-edge NNLS is underdetermined whenever $M > N - 1$ (which is every interesting case). The feasible set $\{r \geq 0 : A\, r = y\}$ is a high-dimensional polytope with many solutions. Lawson–Hanson's active-set algorithm picks a vertex of that polytope — the sparsest feasible $r$. That vertex is algorithm-dependent and has no Bayesian meaning.

Junction-shrinkage adds $(n_j - 1)$ Helmert rows per junction to the NNLS system, scaled by $\sqrt{\lambda}$. The Helmert rows encode the within-junction variance penalty in a way NNLS can solve. The augmented system has

\[ N_{\mathrm{aug}} \;=\; (N - 1) \,+\, \sum_{j} (n_j - 1) \;=\; (N - 1) \,+\, (M - K_{\mathrm{junc}}) \]

rows against $M$ columns. For any realistic gene $N > K_{\mathrm{junc}}$, so $N_{\mathrm{aug}} > M$ — the augmented system is overdetermined. NNLS returns the unique least-squares solution; no vertex-picking, no algorithmic sparsity. That's the airtight part.

Geometric picture. Among all $r$ in the feasible polytope, $\lambda \to 0^+$ selects the unique one with the smallest within-junction variance — the polytope point closest to the "pooled subspace" $\{r_k = \rho_{j(k)} \text{ for all } k\}$. This is a principled point to pick because, under the Mixed-Dirichlet prior, most of the prior mass lives near the pooled subspace (rates on edges sharing junction $j$ are related by a shared $\rho_j$ factor). Junction-shrinkage picks the best-fitting rate vector that's closest to this high-prior-mass region.

See §13 below for a concrete worked example on DADADA.

Ridge NNLS ($L_2$ toward 0). \[ \widehat r \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{r \geq 0} \; \bigl\lVert A\, r - y \bigr\rVert_2^2 \,+\, \lambda \cdot \lVert r \rVert_2^2. \] Solved equivalently as NNLS on the augmented system $\bigl[A;\, \sqrt{\lambda}\, I\bigr]\, r = \bigl[y;\, 0\bigr]$. Shrinks all rates smoothly toward zero; no arbitrary zeros. Appropriate when you want a unique non-sparse answer and are willing to accept biased-toward-zero estimates.
LASSO NNLS ($L_1$ penalty). Because $r \geq 0$, $\lVert r \rVert_1 = \mathbf{1}^\top r$: \[ \widehat r \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{r \geq 0} \; \bigl\lVert A\, r - y \bigr\rVert_2^2 \,+\, \lambda \cdot \mathbf{1}^\top r. \] Coordinate-descent solver. The sparsity is explicit and tuned by $\lambda$. Appropriate when biology justifies sparse rate structure (e.g., most edges negligible) and you want that assumption visible to the reader of the paper.
Smooth NNLS ($L_2$ toward $R_0$). Tikhonov regularisation pulling rates toward a biological reference $R_0$: \[ \widehat r \;=\; \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{r \geq 0} \; \bigl\lVert A\, r - y \bigr\rVert_2^2 \,+\, \lambda \cdot \lVert r - R_0 \cdot \mathbf{1} \rVert_2^2. \] Solved as NNLS on $\bigl[A;\, \sqrt{\lambda}\, I\bigr]\, r = \bigl[y;\, \sqrt{\lambda}\, R_0\, \mathbf{1}\bigr]$. Appropriate when rates are a priori expected near a typical value (log-normal mode) rather than near zero. Shrinks toward $R_0$ as $\lambda$ increases.

Under the SRV framework, each solver is optimal in a sub-region of $\theta$-space, and the goal is to map those sub-regions explicitly. The identifiability table at the end of §8 is a first draft of that map.

Per-edge identifiability score

For each edge $k$ included in the solve, a scaled diagonal of the pseudoinverse gives a continuous identifiability score:

$$ M \;=\; A^\top A + \varepsilon\, I \quad (\varepsilon \approx 10^{-6} \cdot M_{00}), \qquad \mathrm{score}_k \;=\; \frac{1}{M^{-1}_{kk} \cdot \lVert a_k \rVert_2^2} \;\in\; [0, 1]. $$

A column whose direction is redundant with other columns has a large $M^{-1}_{kk}$, driving the score toward zero — red edges when Color transitions by how well they're determined is on. Green edges are uniquely pinned.

Conditioning

  • $\mathrm{rank}(A)$: the number of linearly independent columns. If $\mathrm{rank} < n_{\mathrm{cols}}$, there are missing constraints (positive Nullity in the diagnostics bar) and per-edge NNLS returns one min-norm non-negative feasible point rather than the unique truth.
  • Condition number $\kappa(A) = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ (estimated by power iteration on $A^\top A$). Shown as $\log_{10}\kappa$. Values above $\sim 6$ are noise-sensitive even when nominally full-rank — the numerical ceiling at high $K$.

11. R², residual, and what "rates recovered" means

Given the true rate vector $r^*$ and the recovered $\widehat r$, we report two very different $R^2$ numbers.

Masked R² (the diagnostics bar and right scatter)

Restricted to edges that are observable and whose true rate is above trace level:

$$ \tau_{\mathrm{trace}} \;=\; \max\bigl(10^{-6},\ \textstyle\max_k r^*_k \cdot 10^{-3}\bigr), \qquad \mathrm{mask}_k \;=\; \mathrm{obs}[k] \,\land\, (r^*_k > \tau_{\mathrm{trace}}), $$

$$ R^2_{\mathrm{mask}} \;=\; 1 \,-\, \frac{\sum_k (\widehat r_k - r^*_k)^2}{\sum_k (r^*_k - \overline{r^*})^2} \quad \text{over masked } k. $$

This answers: of the rates we can actually see, how well do we recover them? When the system is full-rank and well-conditioned, $R^2_{\mathrm{mask}} \to 1$.

All-edges R² (left scatter)

Over every edge in the topology, including unobservable ones. Unobservable edges typically get pegged to 0 by NNLS (no data, no basis to set them), so if the true rate happened to be non-zero this number drops. The gap between the two $R^2$ values is the size of the "invisible" rate mass.

Residual

$$ \mathrm{residual} \;=\; \lVert A\, \widehat r - c \rVert_2. $$

Under matched export and enough constraints, NNLS drives this to machine precision. A large residual with low $R^2$ tells you the solver can't even fit the observations — likely a model-mismatch scenario.

12. Reading the visualisation

The DAG

  • Nodes are isoform states, laid out by layer (vertical) and label (horizontal). Node size and colour encode $p_i$.
  • Edges are splicing transitions, width $\propto$ true rate. Turn on Overlay recovered rates (dashed) to compare to $\widehat r$.
  • Color transitions by how well they're determined repaints edges with the identifiability score.
  • Show all nodes & edges reveals every edge in the DADA skeleton regardless of activity — useful for hand-editing zero-rate edges. By default only active, observable edges render.
  • Click any edge or any True-rate cell in the table to edit that rate by hand.

Sashimi panel

Hover a node to draw its splice pattern as arcs over the D/A track — the shape of the mature mRNA for that state.

Scatter plots

  • All edges (left): true vs recovered over every edge. $R^2$ is computed on every point plotted.
  • Observable edges only (right): the same scatter restricted to the observable mask. Its $R^2$ is what you see in the diagnostics bar.

Diagnostics bar

Data equations $=$ number of observable balance rows. Rates to recover $=$ number of usable columns. Independent constraints $=$ rank. Missing constraints $=$ cols $-$ rank. Noise sensitivity $= \log_{10}\kappa$. Unspliced fraction $= p_0$.

13. Worked example — the four solvers on a Mixed DADADA

The best way to see what junction-shrinkage actually does is to watch all four solvers recover the same rate vector. What to notice. Per-edge NNLS leaves some edges at zero — the Lawson–Hanson vertex it picks is the sparsest feasible solution, so edges that are actually active get driven to zero to keep the cardinality low. Pooled NNLS flattens every edge of a junction to the same height: it can't represent the within-junction variation the truth actually has. Junction-shrinkage at small $\lambda$ keeps the edges near their junction's average but lets them vary — it's the closest to truth of the three solvers. Crank $\lambda$ up and it converges on pooled; drag it to the bottom and it starts looking like per-edge.

What the $R^2$ numbers mean. $R^2$ over the observable edges, relative to the ground truth. A solver that recovers every edge exactly has $R^2 = 1$; one that does worse than the mean has $R^2 < 0$. In the Mixed regime, per-edge NNLS is typically below zero (Lawson–Hanson's vertex pick is worse than just guessing the mean); junction-shrinkage at small $\lambda$ is the best of all four because it's the principled polytope-point closest to the pooled subspace.

Why this beats just trusting pooled. Pooled NNLS throws away the within-junction variation that the truth actually has. Junction-shrinkage preserves it — at $\lambda \to 0^+$, the recovered rates still differ across edges of the same junction, just not by as much as an unregularised per-edge NNLS would let them. That's the "soft" in soft-pooling: encode the structural prior without erasing the data's own word.

14. Posterior over active subgraphs — the principled view

IHT (§10) returns a single active subgraph $\widehat S$ as a point estimate. The proper Bayesian object is the posterior distribution $p(S \mid y, \theta)$ — a probability assigned to every active subgraph in light of the observed IPD. This section explains what that distribution looks like, how to compute it, and why it forces a clean conclusion: a single noiseless IPD does not identify the active subgraph, no matter what algorithm you bring.

The Bayesian formulation

By Bayes' rule, the posterior factors as

$$ p(S \mid y, \theta) \;\propto\; p(y \mid S, \theta) \cdot p(S \mid \theta). $$

The prior $p(S \mid \theta)$. Under the Mixed-Dirichlet generator, the active set is independent across child states: each child $c$ with $m_c$ parents picks $k_c = \max(1, \mathrm{round}(\eta \cdot m_c))$ of them uniformly at random. So the prior factors per child:

$$ p(S \mid \theta) \;=\; \prod_c \frac{1}{\binom{m_c}{k_c}} \cdot \mathbb{1}\bigl[\, |S \cap \mathrm{in}(c)| = k_c \,\bigr]. $$

Subgraphs that respect the η-cardinality have equal prior probability; subgraphs that violate it have zero prior. No tree assumption: at $\eta > 0$ children with multiple active parents (diamonds) are explicitly allowed.

The likelihood $p(y \mid S, \theta)$. This is the marginal likelihood of $y$ averaged over rate vectors supported on $S$:

$$ p(y \mid S, \theta) \;=\; \int p(y \mid r) \cdot \pi(r \mid S, \sigma, \alpha) \, dr. $$

Without observation noise, $p(y \mid r) = \delta\bigl(y - f(r)\bigr)$, which is a singular density. We approximate it by a soft-Dirac matched to the residual scale: among rate vectors supported on $S$, find the NNLS-best fit to the steady-state balance system, and score $S$ by

$$ p(y \mid S, \theta) \;\approx\; \exp\!\Bigl(\,-\, \bigl\lVert A_S\, \widehat r_S - c \bigr\rVert_2^2 \,/\, h^2 \,\Bigr) $$

where $A_S$ is the balance matrix restricted to columns in $S$, $c_i = \lambda_i\, p_i$ is the export-weighted RHS, $\widehat r_S = \mathrm{NNLS}(A_S, c)$, and $h$ is a small bandwidth (we use $10^{-3}$). Subgraphs whose best NNLS fit produces a residual at floating-point noise level dominate the posterior; subgraphs with a meaningful residual contribute essentially nothing.

Algorithm

  1. Enumerate every subgraph $S$ satisfying the η-cardinality (factored per child via Cartesian product over $\binom{m_c}{k_c}$ choices).
  2. For each $S$: build $A_S$, solve $\widehat r_S = \mathrm{NNLS}(A_S, c)$, record the residual $\lVert A_S \widehat r_S - c \rVert_2$.
  3. Convert residuals to soft-Dirac evidences and normalise to obtain $p(S \mid y, \theta)$.
  4. Marginalise to get edge-inclusion probabilities $\;P(\text{edge } k \in S \mid y, \theta) = \sum_{S \ni k} p(S \mid y, \theta)$.

Brute-force enumeration is fine for DADADA (≤ 100 subgraphs) and DADADADA at low η (~10² – 10³); larger graphs would need importance sampling or MCMC.

Why this is the right answer (and not a bug)

For each cardinality-consistent subgraph $S$, the balance system $A_S\, r = c$ has a non-empty feasible set $\{r \geq 0 : A_S\, r = c\}$ — and NNLS finds the unique vertex of the minimum cardinality. Whether this set is a single point or a polytope depends on $|S|$ vs. the rank of $A_S$, but as long as some non-negative $r$ produces $A_S\, r = c$, the residual is zero.

For all 96 cardinality-13 subgraphs of DADADA, this is the case: the system has 12 balance rows and 13 columns, but the truth's $f(r)$ uniquely determines $c$, and many $r$-vectors supported on different $S$ produce the same $c$. The forward map $r \mapsto p$ has a non-trivial kernel relative to "rate vectors supported on different cardinality-13 subgraphs."

Geometrically: each cardinality-13 subgraph defines a 13-dimensional polytope in $r$-space; the constraint $A_S\, r = c$ slices out an affine sub-polytope; the intersection with $\mathbb{R}_+^{13}$ is non-empty for many $S$. The data identifies the union of these slices, not the specific one.

Edge-inclusion marginals: cardinality survives, identity doesn't

While the joint posterior is uniform, marginalising over $S$ gives non-trivial edge probabilities. On DADADA Mixed:

The marginal at every edge is exactly $k_c / m_c$. The data gives back the cardinality structure of the prior — and nothing more. Specific edge identities are pure 50/50 (or 1/3, etc.) coin-flips from the data's perspective. This is an honest visualisation of the identifiability ceiling: cardinality is identified; identity is not.

What this says about IHT

IHT (§10 mode "θ-aware") returns a single subgraph from the cardinality-consistent set. From the posterior's perspective, IHT's output is one of the 96 equally-correct candidates — equivalent to flipping fair coins at every choice point. The recovered R² of $\approx 0.4$ on Predetermined isn't IHT failing; it's the average R² over uniformly-distributed wrong-edge picks across the cardinality class. Two IHT runs from different initialisations would land on different subgraphs, both producing the exact same IPD if you forward-simulated them.

IHT is therefore a perfectly fine point estimator in this regime — but it's reporting one draw from a uniform distribution as if it were the answer. The principled object is the distribution itself.

What breaks the degeneracy

  1. Observation noise. A Dirichlet-$\kappa$ likelihood gives differentiable evidence for each $S$. The best-fit residual is no longer zero; subgraphs differ in how naturally their fitted rates fall under the prior $\pi(r \mid S, \sigma, \alpha)$. The posterior concentrates somewhat. Effect size depends on $\kappa$.
  2. Multiple observations. If the same gene is observed under different conditions (perturbations, time-points, mutants) sharing the same active subgraph but different rates, the joint likelihood $\prod_t p(y_t \mid S, \theta_t)$ rapidly prunes $S$. Each new IPD is a new constraint on $S$.
  3. Stronger structural prior. If the prior over $S$ isn't uniform within the cardinality class — e.g., if specific junctions are biologically known to be rarely used — the prior alone breaks ties.
  4. Time-resolved data. Pre-steady-state IPDs (nascent RNA at multiple time-points) carry kinetic information beyond the steady-state, fully constraining individual rates.

Without one of these, single-IPD inference of $S$ is fundamentally degenerate — not because our solvers are weak, but because the inverse problem doesn't contain that information.

Take-aways

  1. Single noiseless IPD → cardinality of the active subgraph is recoverable; specific edge identity is not. Math fact, not an algorithmic limitation.
  2. The marginal edge-inclusion probabilities expose this exactly: they reproduce the prior cardinalities $k_c / m_c$ per child.
  3. IHT is the right point estimator under the cardinality constraint, but its accuracy is bounded above by what the marginal posterior allows — fundamentally less than 1.0 in non-Kinetic regimes.
  4. To do better, the experimental design must change: observation noise breaks the tie weakly, multiple IPDs break it strongly, time-course data breaks it almost entirely.

Assumptions in one place

  1. Steady state. Transcription, splicing, and export have all reached equilibrium. The solver inverts an algebraic system, not a dynamical one.
  2. Deterministic abundances. IPDs are taken as exact — no measurement noise in the forward run. (You can explore noise sensitivity via the condition number and by hand-perturbing rates.)
  3. No nested junctions. Alternative splicing patterns are limited to strictly sequential junctions along the transcript.
  4. Known export kinetics. The solver is told the profile and $\mathrm{nullFrac}$. Real inverse problems have to estimate these or absorb them into the rates.
  5. Scale-invariance. Only the ratio $r / \lambda$ matters for IPDs, and $\lambda_{\mathrm{base}} = 1$ fixes the scale. Doubling every rate and $\lambda_{\mathrm{base}}$ leaves the IPD unchanged.
  6. No back-splicing. Edges go from fewer-junction states to more-junction states only. Splicing is irreversible in this model.
  7. First-order kinetics. All splicing and export reactions are single-step, mass-action rate processes. No cooperativity, no second-order dependences on other species.
